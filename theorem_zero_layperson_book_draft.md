# Theorem Zero: A Beginning

## Subtitle

How a preserved act becomes mathematical structure.

---

# Editorial Note

This rebuilt draft begins under a stricter rule.

Part One establishes.

Part Two reinspects.

The first part will move from the fixed compass opening to the first mathematical field. It will establish the radius, the circle, sixfold closure, the equilateral triangle, the triangular field, native coordinates, algebra, distance classes, the twenty boundary triangles, ratio, area, and invariance.

The second part will not restart the beginning. It will return to established facts as lenses. It will ask what those facts now make visible.

The governing sentence is:

A preserved act becomes structure; a known fact becomes a lens; a lawful change reveals an invariant.

This draft should remain slow, lawful, and continuous. It should not inflate the claim. The first circle does not contain all mathematics as completed content. It gives a lawful beginning from which large regions of mathematics become visible.

# Part One: The First Structure

## Introduction: The First Rule

Begin with one instruction.

Do not change the compass opening.

That is the first discipline. Before there is a theorem, before there is a diagram worth trusting, before there is algebra, there is a preserved act.

Choose a point. Call it O.

Open the compass to some distance.

Draw a circle.

Now move the compass point to the circle. Do not change the opening. Draw again. Step the same distance around the boundary. Let new intersections appear. Let those intersections become stations. Let the same act continue.

The hand moves.

The compass point moves.

The pencil moves.

The drawing changes.

But the opening remains fixed.

That fixed opening is the first invariant.

An invariant is something that remains the same while something else changes. Here, the change is visible. The compass travels from one place to another. The page receives new marks. The construction grows. But the distance carried by the compass is not allowed to alter.

This is the beginning of Theorem Zero.

Not a grand claim.

Not a finished system.

A lawful beginning.

The preserved opening makes equality portable. A length can be carried from the center to the boundary. The same length can become a radius, then a chord, then the side of a triangle, then the edge of a field.

The first act is small, but it is not empty.

Because something is preserved, something can be built.

That is the first idea of this book.

A preserved act becomes structure.

Once a structure exists, it can be inspected. Once something is known about it, that knowledge changes the next inspection. A fact does not merely sit in the mind as a result. It becomes a lens.

This gives the method of the book.

Construct.

Observe.

Name.

Lock the claim.

Reinspect.

The order matters. Mathematics should not arrive here as a list of commands. It should appear as the compact memory of what the construction has already forced.

So the first promise is modest and strict.

We will not say more than the construction allows.

We will not borrow meaning before it has been earned.

We will not treat resemblance as proof.

We will begin with the compass opening, preserve it, and see what follows.

The book is not really about the hexagon, though the hexagon will appear.

It is not really about a circle, though the circle is the first visible form.

It is about invariance becoming structure.

The first rule remains:

Do not change the compass opening.

Everything begins there.

## Step 1: The Fixed Radius and the First Circle

The first construction is simple.

Choose a point and call it O.

Choose a compass opening.

Draw a circle around O.

The compass opening is the radius of the circle. Every point on the circle is the same distance from O. That is what the circle records.

A circle is not only a round mark. It is a distance condition made visible.

If P is any point on the circle, then OP is equal to the radius.

If the radius is called r, then:

OP = r

For this construction, we may use the radius as the native unit. That means we measure the construction by the distance that the compass already preserves.

So we set:

r = 1

This does not mean that every circle has radius 1. It means that this construction takes its own compass opening as its first unit.

Now the first circle can be read clearly.

There is a center.

There is a unit.

There is a boundary.

Every point on the boundary is one unit from the center.

This is the first established fact.

The circle with center O and radius 1 is the set of all points one unit from O.

Why is this true?

Because the compass draws exactly that condition. The point of the compass stays at O while the pencil moves at a fixed distance from O. The pencil may travel around the center, but its distance from the center remains the compass opening.

The circle is therefore the trace of a preserved distance.

This gives the first effect.

Any point on the circle may be treated as a lawful boundary station. It is not just somewhere on the page. It is known to be one unit from O.

That matters because the construction can now continue.

The compass opening has not changed. The same distance that made the radius can be carried to a boundary station. The boundary point can become a new place from which the same act is performed.

So Step 1 establishes three things.

First, the fixed opening is the first unit.

Second, the circle is the visible record of that unit around O.

Third, the boundary is not passive. It supplies lawful stations for the next act.

The construction has not yet produced much.

But it has produced enough.

A preserved distance has become a center, a boundary, and a unit.

That is the first structure.

## Step 2: Sixfold Closure and the Equilateral Triangle

Choose one point on the circle.

Call it A.

Because A lies on the circle centered at O, the distance OA is one unit.

Now place the compass point on A.

Do not change the compass opening.

Draw a second circle with the same radius.

This new circle meets the first circle at two points. Choose one of them and call it B.

Now inspect triangle OAB.

A lies on the circle centered at O, so:

OA = 1

B lies on the circle centered at O, so:

OB = 1

B also lies on the circle centered at A, drawn with the same compass opening, so:

AB = 1

Therefore:

OA = OB = AB = 1

Triangle OAB has three equal sides.

So triangle OAB is equilateral.

This is the first proof lock.

The triangle is not equilateral because it looks equilateral. It is equilateral because the construction gives no other possibility. The point B is one unit from O and one unit from A, while A is already one unit from O.

Three equal distances force the triangle.

This gives the next established fact.

Equal-radius circle intersection produces an equilateral triangle with the two centers.

The effect is immediate.

The compass has not merely drawn a curve. It has carried equality from one place to another. The radius has become a chord. The chord has become the side of a triangle. The same unit now appears in three roles.

Continue the same stepping around the first circle.

From A, the same radius finds B.

From B, the same radius finds the next boundary station.

Then the next.

Then the next.

The same unit chord is stepped around the circle.

The process closes after six equal placements.

Why six?

Each neighboring boundary pair with O forms an equilateral triangle. In an equilateral triangle, the three angles are equal. The angles of a triangle fill a half-turn. So each angle of an equilateral triangle is one third of a half-turn.

That is one sixth of a full turn.

At O, each boundary step advances by that one-sixth turn. After six such turns, the construction has completed the full turn and returned to its starting place.

So the first circle gives six boundary stations.

Call them:

A, B, C, D, E, F

The central triangles are:

OAB

OBC

OCD

ODE

OEF

OFA

Each is equilateral.

This gives the next established fact.

The fixed radius steps around its own circle in six equal placements.

The effect is large.

The circle is no longer only a boundary. It is now a sixfold structure. It has six lawful boundary stations, six unit chords, six central equilateral triangles, and the first visible directions of the field.

The page has begun to organize itself.

Not by decoration.

Not by assumption.

By preserved distance.

## Step 3: The Triangular Field and Native Coordinates

The first circle gives six boundary stations.

But a station is more than a point.

A station is a place from which the same operation can be repeated.

Once A exists, the compass can be placed at A.

Once B exists, the compass can be placed at B.

Once further intersections appear, they can become stations as well.

The rule remains unchanged.

Do not change the compass opening.

Move to a lawful station.

Draw again.

Let new intersections become new stations.

Repeat.

This is how the construction begins to spread.

The result is not random. The same unit repeats. The same equilateral relation repeats. The same edge directions repeat. Triangle meets triangle. Edge meets edge. Point leads to point.

The construction becomes a triangular field.

A field, in this sense, is a structured space in which the same rule can continue to operate. The first circle is the beginning. The field is the continuation.

The smallest repeated body in this field is the equilateral triangle of side length 1. These triangles fit together without gaps or overlaps. Their edges belong to three natural direction families.

One family runs horizontally.

One rises diagonally.

One falls diagonally.

These directions are not imposed from outside. They are produced by the sixfold construction itself.

Once the field exists, position can be described.

But the first coordinate language should be faithful to the field. A square grid uses two directions meeting at a right angle. The triangular field has native directions meeting at 60 degrees.

So we begin with the field's own language.

Choose two unit step directions in the triangular field.

Call them u and v.

Each has length 1.

The angle between them is 60 degrees.

To reach a point, take a steps in the u direction and b steps in the v direction.

The pair (a, b) records that movement.

A coordinate is therefore not just a label. It is a history of lawful steps.

This gives the next established fact.

A point in the triangular field can be described by step counts along two 60-degree unit directions.

Why is this lawful?

Because the field itself is generated by repeated unit steps in those directions. The coordinate does not arrive from outside the construction. It records how the construction reaches the point.

The effect is decisive.

The field can now be counted.

Movement can now be remembered.

Position can now be carried into algebra.

This prepares the next step.

Algebra will not enter as symbol manipulation detached from the drawing. It will enter as memory of construction.

The construction has produced relations.

The next task is to record them without losing their origin.

## Step 4: Algebra as Memory of Construction

The triangular field can now be counted.

A point can be reached by lawful steps.

A coordinate can record those steps.

This is the right moment for algebra to enter.

But algebra should not enter as a separate language dropped onto the construction from above. It should enter as memory.

The construction has already produced relations.

The compass opening gave a unit.

The circle recorded every point one unit from O.

The equal-radius intersection produced an equilateral triangle.

The sixfold closure produced six boundary stations.

The repeated operation produced a triangular field.

Now algebra begins to preserve those relations in compact form.

The first algebra was already present in the equilateral triangle:

OA = OB = AB = 1

That equation did not create the equality.

The compass created the equality.

The equation remembered it.

This is the proper role of algebra in Theorem Zero.

Algebra records what the construction has earned.

Now return to the native coordinates of the triangular field.

Choose two unit directions.

Call them u and v.

Each has length 1.

They meet at 60 degrees.

A point reached by a steps in the u direction and b steps in the v direction can be recorded as:

a*u + b*v

This expression is not decorative. It is a compressed travel history.

It says:

take a lawful steps one way,

take b lawful steps another way,

and arrive at the point.

Now ask the next question.

How far is that point from the starting point?

On a square grid, the usual distance rule comes from two directions meeting at a right angle. But the triangular field does not begin with a right angle. Its native directions meet at 60 degrees.

So the square-grid distance rule is not the first native rule here.

The triangular field needs a distance rule that remembers its own structure.

The rule is:

distance squared = a^2 + ab + b^2

This is one of the central algebraic facts of the construction.

It needs a proof lock.

Place the first unit direction across the page:

u = (1, 0)

Place the second unit direction at the lawful 60-degree angle:

v = (1/2, sqrt(3)/2)

Now take a steps in the u direction and b steps in the v direction.

The across-part is:

a + b/2

The up-part is:

b*sqrt(3)/2

So the ordinary across-and-up coordinates of the point are:

(a + b/2, b*sqrt(3)/2)

To find the squared distance from the starting point, square the across-part and the up-part, then add them:

(a + b/2)^2 + (b*sqrt(3)/2)^2

Now expand the first square:

(a + b/2)^2 = a^2 + ab + b^2/4

Expand the second square:

(b*sqrt(3)/2)^2 = 3b^2/4

Add them:

a^2 + ab + b^2/4 + 3b^2/4

The two b^2 parts combine:

b^2/4 + 3b^2/4 = b^2

So the squared distance is:

a^2 + ab + b^2

That is the native distance memory of the triangular field.

The term a^2 remembers the first direction.

The term b^2 remembers the second direction.

The middle term ab remembers that the two directions are not independent at a right angle. They meet at 60 degrees. They lean into one another.

The middle term is not an inconvenience.

It is fidelity.

It keeps the algebra loyal to the construction.

This gives the next established fact.

In the triangular field, the squared distance associated with native step counts a and b is:

a^2 + ab + b^2

Why is this lawful?

Because the two native unit directions meet at 60 degrees, and the distance rule must preserve that relation.

The effect is important.

The field can now be searched by distance.

The eye can see nearby relations, but algebra can carry the relation farther. A drawing may become crowded. A diagram may become imperfect. The algebra remembers what the construction means even when the picture is no longer enough.

This is not a departure from geometry.

It is geometry made portable.

The construction gives the relation.

Algebra preserves it.

Now the field can classify its own distances.

That is the next step.

## Step 5: Distance Classes in the Field

The distance rule lets the field begin to classify itself.

A point in the triangular field can be reached by step counts:

(a, b)

The squared distance from the starting point is:

a^2 + ab + b^2

This rule is not only a way to calculate.

It is a way to recognize relations.

Different movements through the field may produce the same distance. When they do, they belong to the same distance class.

A distance class is a family of point-pairs that share the same distance.

Now test the first cases.

If a = 1 and b = 0, then:

1^2 + 1*0 + 0^2 = 1

So the squared distance is 1.

The distance is:

1

That is one unit step in the first native direction.

If a = 0 and b = 1, then:

0^2 + 0*1 + 1^2 = 1

The distance is again:

1

That is one unit step in the second native direction.

So the unit distance is not trapped in one direction. It appears in each native direction of the field.

The direction changes.

The length does not.

That is the first distance class.

Now take one step in each native direction.

Let a = 1 and b = 1.

Then:

1^2 + 1*1 + 1^2 = 3

So the squared distance is 3.

The distance is:

sqrt(3)

This distance appears when the two native directions combine. It is not a single unit edge. It is a relation produced by moving through both directions of the triangular field.

Now take two steps in one straight native direction.

Let a = 2 and b = 0.

Then:

2^2 + 2*0 + 0^2 = 4

So the squared distance is 4.

The distance is:

2

This is a straight two-unit extension.

The first distance classes are therefore:

1

sqrt(3)

2

These are not just numbers.

They are movement types.

Distance 1 records adjacency.

Distance sqrt(3) records a combined-direction relation.

Distance 2 records straight extension.

The field is beginning to sort itself.

Before algebra, the eye could notice short segments and longer segments. But the eye alone is not enough. The drawing may be imperfect. The field may grow too large to inspect by sight. Algebra gives a stable test.

The expression:

a^2 + ab + b^2

allows the construction to recognize distance wherever the relevant step relation appears.

This is the next established fact.

The early distance classes of the triangular field include:

1

sqrt(3)

2

Why is this lawful?

Because these distances arise directly from the first small step-count combinations in the native distance rule.

The effect is decisive.

Segments can now be classified.

Point-pairs can now be compared.

Triangles can now be sorted by side relations.

This prepares the next major inspection.

At the beginning, the central triangles were all equilateral. Each used the center O and two neighboring boundary stations. Each had side length 1.

But the field and the first circle contain more than those six central triangles.

Once distance classes are known, other triangles can be read more clearly.

Some triangles use unit sides.

Some use distance sqrt(3).

Some use distance 2.

Some contain an opposite pair of boundary stations.

Some contain a right angle.

Some preserve equilateral identity at a larger scale.

Classification has begun.

The construction has moved from drawing figures to recognizing families.

This is the effect of Step 5.

The field is no longer only a pattern of points and lines.

It is a searchable structure.

The next task is to return to the first circle and inspect its six boundary stations again.

This time, the question will not be:

what points are there?

The question will be:

what triangles are already available when three boundary stations are chosen?

## Step 6: The Twenty Boundary Triangles

Return to the first circle.

The construction has moved outward into the triangular field. It has produced native coordinates and a distance rule. It has begun to classify distances.

Now those facts become lenses.

The first circle can be inspected again.

It has six boundary stations:

A, B, C, D, E, F

Until now, these stations have mostly served the central construction. Each neighboring pair, together with O, forms an equilateral triangle:

OAB

OBC

OCD

ODE

OEF

OFA

But the center is not the only point that can be used.

The six boundary stations can be selected among themselves.

Choose any three of them.

Those three boundary stations form a triangle.

So the question becomes:

how many boundary-point triangles are available in the first circle?

There are six stations.

A triangle needs three of them.

So the count is:

C(6, 3) = 20

The first circle contains twenty boundary-point triangles.

This is the next established fact.

It is not a metaphor. It is a finite catalogue.

Now the more important question appears:

what kinds of triangles are they?

At first, twenty triangles may sound like twenty different cases. But the construction is more ordered than that. The twenty triangles collapse into three identity families.

The proof lock is simple.

When three boundary stations are chosen from the six, those stations divide the circular boundary into three gaps.

Each gap is counted in boundary steps.

The three gap counts must be positive whole numbers.

Together, they must add to 6, because the full boundary has six equal steps.

Up to rotation and reversal, there are only three possible gap patterns:

1,1,4

1,2,3

2,2,2

These three gap patterns give the three triangle families.

The pattern 1,1,4 means three consecutive stations.

For example:

A, B, C

The sides AB and BC are unit chords. The third side AC is longer. This gives an isosceles triangle with angle pattern:

30-120-30

There are six such triangles, one beginning at each boundary station:

ABC

BCD

CDE

DEF

EFA

FAB

So the first family has count:

6

The pattern 2,2,2 means alternating stations.

For example:

A, C, E

The chosen stations are evenly spaced around the circle. The resulting triangle is equilateral.

The other alternating triangle is:

B, D, F

So the second family has count:

2

Its angle pattern is:

60-60-60

The pattern 1,2,3 means that one pair of chosen stations is opposite.

For example:

A, B, D

A and D are opposite boundary stations. Therefore AD is a diameter.

Since the radius is 1, the diameter has length:

2

The triangle also contains a unit chord, such as AB.

These diameter-containing triangles form the third family.

There are three opposite pairs:

A and D

B and E

C and F

For each opposite pair, there are four possible third boundary stations.

So the count is:

3 * 4 = 12

These twelve triangles are the 30-60-90 family. The right angle and side pattern will be locked in the next step.

Now the count closes:

6 + 2 + 12 = 20

So the twenty boundary triangles collapse into:

6 triangles of type 30-120-30

2 triangles of type 60-60-60

12 triangles of type 30-60-90

This gives the next established fact.

Choosing any three of the six boundary stations produces exactly twenty boundary triangles, and these twenty fall into three identity families.

Why is this lawful?

Because every triple of boundary stations creates one of the three possible gap patterns:

1,1,4

1,2,3

2,2,2

The effect is important.

The first circle is no longer only a boundary.

It is no longer only the outline of the first sixfold construction.

It is now a compact catalogue of triangle identities.

This must be said carefully.

The first circle does not contain all later mathematics as completed content. It does not prove everything. It does not remove the need for later construction.

The lawful claim is smaller and stronger.

The first circle contains a seed catalogue of triangle identities that the wider field can repeat, reorient, translate, reflect, scale, and recombine.

That is enough.

It is exact.

It is visible.

It is earned.

The next step is to inspect the most important of these families: the diameter-containing triangle.

That family opens the right angle.

The right angle opens the 30-60-90 side pattern.

The side pattern opens ratio.

And ratio will later open trigonometry.

But the next step must stay with the construction.

First, the right angle has to be earned.

## Step 7: Height, Right Angle, and the 30-60-90 Triangle

The twenty boundary triangles give one family special importance.

The diameter-containing family.

A representative example is triangle ABD.

A and D are opposite boundary stations.

So AD passes through the center O.

Since OA is one radius and OD is one radius, AD is a diameter.

The radius is 1, so:

AD = 2

A and B are neighboring boundary stations.

So AB is a unit chord.

Therefore:

AB = 1

So triangle ABD already contains two known lengths:

AB = 1

AD = 2

But to understand the triangle properly, one more fact has to be locked.

Triangle ABD is a right triangle.

This needs care.

There are two related issues here, and they should not be blurred.

First, the right angle must be shown to be native to the construction.

Second, this particular boundary triangle must be shown to contain one.

The height of an equilateral triangle helps with the first issue.

Begin with an equilateral triangle.

Its three sides are equal.

If a line is drawn from one vertex to the midpoint of the opposite side, the equilateral triangle is divided into two matching halves.

Each half has the same hypotenuse, the same half-base, and the same dividing line.

The two halves mirror one another.

Because the base has been split into two equal opposite directions, the dividing line cannot lean toward one side more than the other. It meets the base squarely.

That square meeting is a right angle.

So the height of an equilateral triangle shows that the right angle is not foreign to the triangular construction. It can appear inside a figure generated by equal sides and symmetry.

But that does not yet prove that triangle ABD is right-angled.

For ABD, the locking fact is the diameter relation.

In triangle ABD, the side AD is a diameter of the circle, and B is a point on the same circle.

A point on a circle sees a diameter under a right angle.

So the angle at B is a right angle.

The height argument shows that right angles belong naturally inside the construction.

The diameter argument locks this specific boundary triangle as right-angled.

So triangle ABD can now be read as a right triangle with hypotenuse AD.

Now the remaining side can be found.

We know:

AB = 1

AD = 2

The unknown side is BD.

Because ABD is a right triangle, the two shorter sides square to the longest side.

So:

AB^2 + BD^2 = AD^2

Substitute the known lengths:

1^2 + BD^2 = 2^2

So:

1 + BD^2 = 4

Therefore:

BD^2 = 3

So:

BD = sqrt(3)

The side pattern of triangle ABD is therefore:

1 : sqrt(3) : 2

This is the next established fact.

The 30-60-90 triangle has side pattern:

1 : sqrt(3) : 2

Why is this lawful?

Because the triangle contains a unit chord, a diameter of length 2, and a right angle. Once those are established, the remaining side is forced.

The effect is large.

The first circle has now produced a triangle that carries three different lengths:

1

sqrt(3)

2

These are the same early distance classes that appeared in the triangular field.

But now they are gathered into one triangle identity.

The construction began with 60-degree relations. The equilateral triangle and the sixfold circle made that natural. But the right angle has now appeared from within the same construction.

This matters.

The right angle is not foreign to Theorem Zero.

It is not imported as a square-grid assumption.

It is earned through the equilateral height and through the diameter relation.

Once the right angle is available, a new kind of comparison becomes possible.

In a right triangle, one side can be compared with another in a stable way.

The shortest side can be compared with the longest side.

The middle side can be compared with the longest side.

The middle side can be compared with the shortest side.

For the 30-60-90 triangle:

the shortest side is 1

the middle side is sqrt(3)

the longest side is 2

So:

1 is half of 2

sqrt(3) is sqrt(3)/2 of 2

sqrt(3) is sqrt(3) times 1

These are ratios.

A ratio is not a new length.

A ratio is a comparison between lengths.

This gives the next opening.

The 30-60-90 triangle is not only a shape. It is a ratio-bearing structure.

The triangle identity now has a side pattern that can survive growth.

That is the doorway to scaling.

And scaling is the doorway to trigonometry.

But again, the order matters.

First the right angle.

Then the side pattern.

Then ratio.

Then scaling.

Then the naming of stable ratios.

The next step is therefore not to memorize trigonometry.

It is to understand why ratios can remain stable when a triangle changes size.

## Step 8: Ratio, Scaling, and Trigonometric Affordance

The 30-60-90 triangle has now been locked.

Its side pattern is:

1 : sqrt(3) : 2

That pattern appeared first in a particular triangle, such as ABD.

But the pattern is not trapped inside that one instance.

The field can build larger versions of the same triangle identity.

A 30-60-90 triangle may have side lengths:

1 : sqrt(3) : 2

A larger one may have:

2 : 2sqrt(3) : 4

Another may have:

3 : 3sqrt(3) : 6

Another may have:

10 : 10sqrt(3) : 20

The absolute lengths change.

The ratios do not.

The shortest side is still half the hypotenuse.

The middle side is still sqrt(3)/2 of the hypotenuse.

The middle side is still sqrt(3) times the shortest side.

This is similarity.

Similarity means same shape, with size allowed to change.

A similar triangle preserves its angle pattern and its corresponding side ratios.

It does not necessarily preserve absolute length.

This distinction matters.

A larger 30-60-90 triangle is not the same size as the first one. It is not congruent to the first one unless the scale factor is 1.

But it is similar.

The shape remains.

The angle pattern remains.

The ratios remain.

This gives the next established fact.

Scaling a triangle changes its absolute lengths, but preserves its corresponding side ratios when all lengths are multiplied by the same factor.

Why is this lawful?

Because multiplying every side by the same factor changes the unit of size but not the comparison between corresponding sides.

If both sides in a comparison are doubled, their ratio remains the same.

If both are tripled, their ratio remains the same.

The scale changes the lengths.

It does not change the proportion.

This is the bridge to trigonometry.

Trigonometry should not enter first as a table.

It should not enter first as a calculator procedure.

It should enter as the naming of stable ratios inside a right-triangle identity.

In a right triangle, choose one acute angle.

One side lies opposite that angle.

One side lies beside that angle. This is the adjacent side.

The longest side lies opposite the right angle. This is the hypotenuse.

There are three basic comparisons.

Sine compares the opposite side with the hypotenuse.

Cosine compares the adjacent side with the hypotenuse.

Tangent compares the opposite side with the adjacent side.

So:

sin(angle) = opposite / hypotenuse

cos(angle) = adjacent / hypotenuse

tan(angle) = opposite / adjacent

These are not new lengths.

They are ratios.

They matter because they survive scaling.

Now apply this to the 30-60-90 triangle.

For the 30-degree angle, the side opposite the angle is 1.

The adjacent side is sqrt(3).

The hypotenuse is 2.

So:

sin(30) = 1/2

cos(30) = sqrt(3)/2

tan(30) = 1/sqrt(3)

For the 60-degree angle, the opposite and adjacent sides swap.

So:

sin(60) = sqrt(3)/2

cos(60) = 1/2

tan(60) = sqrt(3)

These values are not memorized first.

They are consequences of the triangle identity.

The construction gives the triangle.

The triangle gives the side pattern.

The side pattern gives the ratios.

The ratios receive names.

This gives the next established fact.

Sine, cosine, and tangent name stable ratios inside a right triangle.

Why is this lawful?

Because similar right triangles preserve the side comparisons associated with the same acute angle.

The effect is significant.

An angle can now be read as a source of stable ratio.

This prepares projection.

If a unit segment leans away from the horizontal direction, it can be asked two questions.

How much of the unit runs across?

How much of the unit rises?

Those two parts are projections.

For a unit segment leaning at an angle t:

## Chapter 17: Proof by Recomposition

A body can now be opened.

That is the new fact.

The central hexagon can be read as six equilateral cells.

A rhombus can be read as two equilateral cells.

A larger triangle can be read as many smaller cells.

A parallelogram can be read as a composition of triangular parts.

Once this is possible, proof changes.

A proof does not always have to measure a figure directly.

Sometimes a figure can be taken apart and rebuilt into another figure whose measure is easier to see.

If the same pieces are present before and after, and if no piece has been stretched, lost, duplicated, or overlapped, then the area has not changed.

The outline may change.

The arrangement may change.

The visibility of the measure may change.

But the surface remains.

This is proof by recomposition.

It is not a trick.

It is invariance made visible.

---

Begin with a simple triangle.

Take any triangle in the field whose base and height are known.

Make a second copy of the same triangle.

Turn or reflect the second copy so that the two triangles fit together along matching sides.

Together, the two triangles form a parallelogram.

The parallelogram has the same base as the triangle.

Its height is the same height.

The parallelogram's area is:

base * height

The original triangle is half of that parallelogram.

So the triangle's area is:

1/2 * base * height

This formula should not arrive first.

The recomposition arrives first.

The triangle is doubled.

The two copies form a parallelogram.

The parallelogram area is easier to see.

Then the triangle is half of it.

So the formula records the recomposition:

area of triangle = 1/2 * base * height

The proof is not merely algebraic.

It is geometric accounting.

The same pieces explain the measure.

---

This same proof has already been hiding inside the unit equilateral cell.

The unit equilateral triangle has base 1.

Its height is:

sqrt(3)/2

So its area is:

1/2 * 1 * sqrt(3)/2

That gives:

sqrt(3)/4

But now the formula is better understood.

The factor 1/2 is not an arbitrary instruction.

It comes from the fact that two matching triangles form a parallelogram.

The base and height belong to the parallelogram.

The triangle is half of that body.

So the area formula is a memory of recomposition.

Again, algebra records construction.

It records not only the shape, but the way the shape can be doubled into another lawful body.

---

Now take the rhombus.

A rhombus in the triangular field is made from two equilateral cells.

Each cell has area:

sqrt(3)/4

So the rhombus has area:

2 * sqrt(3)/4

That is:

sqrt(3)/2

But the rhombus can also be read as a parallelogram.

Its base is one unit.

Its height is:

sqrt(3)/2

So its area is:

1 * sqrt(3)/2

That is again:

sqrt(3)/2

The two readings agree.

One reading counts cells.

The other reading uses base and height.

This agreement matters.

It shows that the formula is not floating separately from the construction. The cell-count and the base-height relation converge on the same measure.

The body has been read in two lawful ways.

The result is stable.

That stability is another kind of proof.

Now take the central hexagon.

sqrt(3)/4

6 * sqrt(3)/4

3sqrt(3)/2

This is a direct cell-counting proof.

But the hexagon also supports recomposition.

Its six triangular cells can be grouped into three rhombi.

Each rhombus contains two equilateral cells.

Each rhombus has area:

sqrt(3)/2

Three such rhombi have area:

3sqrt(3)/2

So again the readings agree.

Six triangles.

Three rhombi.

One hexagon.

The same total area appears through different lawful decompositions.

The figure has not changed.

The partition has changed.

The invariant is area.

---

This gives the next established fact.

A figure may have more than one lawful decomposition, and those decompositions must agree in total area if the same surface is being counted.

Why is this true?

Because decomposition changes the reading of the body, not the body itself. If the parts fill the same region without gaps or overlaps, the total area remains the area of the original region.

The effect is important.

Proof can now compare readings.

One decomposition may reveal a count.

Another may reveal a formula.

Another may reveal symmetry.

Another may reveal a relation between figures.

The body becomes a site of converging evidence.

When two lawful readings give the same result, the construction strengthens.

---

Proof by recomposition also clarifies why cutting must be disciplined.

A bad cut can obscure structure.

A careless rearrangement can smuggle in an error.

If pieces overlap, area has been counted twice.

If a gap appears, area has been lost.

If a piece is stretched, it is no longer the same piece.

If a piece is duplicated, the figure has gained surface that was not present before.

So every recomposition must answer four questions.

Are the pieces unchanged?

Are all pieces still present?

Is any piece counted twice?

Does the new arrangement fill the intended region exactly?

If the answers are secure, area can be preserved through the change.

If not, the proof fails.

This is not merely a technical warning.

It is part of the book's discipline.

No affordance without construction.

No preservation without a named invariant.

No proof by picture unless the picture is locked by a reason.
