Step00

Introduction: the fixed act

This book begins with one instruction.

Do not change the compass opening.

Choose a center. Open the compass to some distance. Draw a circle. Place the compass point on the circle. Without changing the opening, step that same distance around the boundary.

The hand moves. The compass point moves. The pencil moves. The drawing changes. But the opening remains fixed.

That fixed opening is the first invariant. An invariant is something that remains the same while something else changes.

Because the opening remains itself, equality becomes portable. A length can be carried. A radius can become a chord. A chord can become the side of a triangle. A point can become a station. A station can become the center of another circle.

The first lesson

A preserved act can become a structure.

The book is not really about the hexagon, though the hexagon appears. It is about invariance becoming structure. Construction first, observation second, name third, mathematics fourth.

Step01

The fixed radius and the first circle

Choose a point. Call it O. Choose a compass opening. Draw a circle around O.

A circle is not just a round line. It is a distance condition made visible. Every point on the circle is the same distance from the center.

OP = r

If the construction uses its own radius as its native unit, set r = 1. This does not mean every circle has radius 1. It means this construction measures itself by the distance the compass preserves.

Ledger entry

A circle with center O and radius 1 is the set of all points one unit from O.

What it opens

The circle is no longer only a boundary. It is a set of lawful stations. Any point on the circle can become a place from which the same preserved opening can act again.

Step02

Sixfold closure and the equilateral triangle

Choose one point on the circle. Call it A. Place the compass point on A. Do not change the opening. Draw again.

The new circle meets the first at B. Now inspect triangle OAB. A lies one radius from O. B lies one radius from O. B also lies one radius from A.

OA = OB = AB = 1

Triangle OAB is therefore equilateral. It is not equilateral because it looks right. It is equilateral because the construction gives no other possibility.

Repeat the same step around the circle. Each neighboring boundary pair with O forms another equilateral triangle. The process closes after six equal placements.

Why six? In an equilateral triangle, the three equal angles fill a half-turn. Each is one third of a half-turn, or one sixth of a full turn. Six such turns complete the circle.

Ledger entry

The fixed radius steps around its own circle in six equal placements, producing six boundary stations and six central equilateral triangles.

Step03

The triangular field and native coordinates

A station is more than a point. It is a place from which the same operation can be repeated.

Once A exists, the compass can be placed at A. Once B exists, the compass can be placed at B. New intersections become further lawful stations. The same unit repeats. Triangle touches triangle. Point leads to point. The construction becomes a triangular field.

A field, here, is a structured space in which a rule can continue to operate. Its natural step directions meet at 60 degrees, not 90 degrees.

Choose two unit step directions in the field. Call them u and v. Each has length 1. The angle between them is 60 degrees. To reach a point, take a steps in direction u and b steps in direction v.

Ledger entry

A point in the triangular field can be described by step counts along two 60-degree unit directions. A coordinate is a history of lawful movement.

Step04

Algebra as memory of construction

Algebra should not enter as a foreign language. It should enter as memory.

The equation OA = OB = AB = 1 did not create equality. The compass created the equality. The equation recorded it.

Let u and v be the two native step directions. Each has length 1, and they meet at 60 degrees. A point reached by a steps in u and b steps in v is recorded as:

a u + b v

The native squared-distance rule is:

a^2 + ab + b^2

The middle term remembers the 60-degree relation. It is not an inconvenience. It is fidelity. It keeps the algebra loyal to the construction.

Ledger entry

In the triangular field, squared distance associated with native step counts a and b is a^2 + ab + b^2.

Step05

Distance classes in the field

The distance rule lets the field classify itself.

If a = 1 and b = 0, the squared distance is 1. If a = 0 and b = 1, it is also 1. These are unit steps.

If a = 1 and b = 1, then:

1^2 + 1\cdot1 + 1^2 = 3

The distance is sqrt(3). If a = 2 and b = 0, the distance is 2.

The first distance classes are 1, sqrt(3), and 2. These are not merely numbers. They are movement types: adjacency, combined-direction relation, and straight extension.

What it opens

Segments and triangles can now be classified by lawful length relations.

Step06

The twenty boundary triangles

Return to the first circle. It has six boundary stations: A, B, C, D, E, and F.

Choose any three boundary stations. Those three stations form a triangle. From six points, the number of ways to choose three is:

C(6,3)=20

At first this sounds like twenty separate cases. But the twenty collapse into three identity families. Three chosen stations divide the circular boundary into three positive whole-number gaps that sum to 6. Up to rotation and reversal, only three gap patterns exist: 1,1,4; 1,2,3; and 2,2,2.

These give six 30-120-30 triangles, twelve 30-60-90 triangles, and two 60-60-60 triangles.

Ledger entry

The first circle is a compact catalogue of boundary-triangle identities. The lawful claim is not that it contains all mathematics as finished content. It contains a seed catalogue the wider field can repeat, reorient, translate, reflect, scale, and recombine.

Step07

Height, right angle, and the 30-60-90 triangle

Take a representative diameter-containing triangle, such as ABD.

A and D are opposite boundary stations, so AD is a diameter. Since the radius is 1, AD has length 2. A and B are neighboring boundary stations, so AB has length 1.

The right angle must be locked carefully. The height of an equilateral triangle shows that right angles are native to the construction. But for ABD, the locking fact is the diameter relation: a point on a circle sees a diameter under a right angle.

So ABD is a right triangle with hypotenuse AD.

AB^2 + BD^2 = AD^2
1^2 + BD^2 = 2^2
BD = \sqrt{3}

Ledger entry

The 30-60-90 triangle has side pattern 1 : sqrt(3) : 2.

Step08

Ratio, scaling, and trigonometric affordance

The 30-60-90 triangle has side pattern 1 : sqrt(3) : 2.

A larger version may have side pattern 2 : 2sqrt(3) : 4, or 3 : 3sqrt(3) : 6. The absolute lengths change. The ratios do not.

This is similarity: same shape, different possible size. A similar triangle preserves its angle pattern and corresponding side ratios.

Trigonometry begins when stable ratios inside a right-triangle identity are named.

\sin(30)=1/2,\quad \cos(30)=\sqrt{3}/2
\sin(60)=\sqrt{3}/2,\quad \cos(60)=1/2

For a unit segment leaning at angle t, cosine is the across-part and sine is the up-part. The original circle can now be read again: not only as points one unit from O, but as all possible leans of a unit radius.

(\cos(t),\sin(t))
Step09

Area and cell-counting

So far, the native unit has mostly been length. The triangular field also gives a unit of surface.

The smallest equilateral triangle becomes an area cell. Area begins not with a formula, but with counting: one cell, two cells, six cells around the center, larger bodies assembled from repeated cells.

If the side length of a unit equilateral cell is 1, its height is sqrt(3)/2, so its area is:

\frac12 \cdot 1 \cdot \frac{\sqrt3}{2}=\frac{\sqrt3}{4}

A rhombus made from two cells has area sqrt(3)/2. The central hexagon made from six cells has area 3sqrt(3)/2.

What it opens

Rearrangement becomes meaningful. A figure may change outline while preserving area, provided its pieces are not stretched, lost, duplicated, or overlapped.

Step10

Transformations, identity, and invariance

The first part returns to its deepest question: what can change, and what must remain?

A transformation is a permitted change. An invariant is what the change is not allowed to destroy.

Rotation changes position around a center while preserving distance and angle. Reflection reverses handedness while preserving length and angle size. Translation changes location while preserving local relation. Scaling changes size while preserving ratio and angle pattern. Rearrangement changes outline while preserving area when the pieces are handled lawfully.

Different transformations preserve different kinds of truth.

The fixed compass opening was the first preserved truth. Because it remained fixed, equality became portable. Because equality became portable, the first circle became meaningful. Because the circle supplied stations, equilateral triangles appeared. Because the step repeated, the triangular field appeared. Because the field had directions, coordinates and algebra became possible.

Closing movement

A lawful change is known by what it preserves. The construction began with one preserved distance. It now opens a broader idea: mathematics studies preserved truth through lawful change.
